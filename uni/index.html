Online gambling businesses face the growing majority of customer complaints in Britain’s gambling industry amid a climate of increased regulation and fines, an analysis of a decade’s worth of company regulatory return data acquired via FOI from the UK Gambling Commission has revealed.

“We collect a wide range of data as regulatory returns. We use this to monitor compliance with various requirements, ensure that processes are working correctly, to inform policy development and to understand how the gambling industry is working more widely. We may take action depending on our findings, for example, as a result of analysing the complaints data we’ve received from operators in the regulatory returns we are proposing to change wording of the regulatory returns so the data is more accurate.“

“Growing complaint numbers in a sector can either be a sign of increased dissatisfaction or can indicate that complaints processes are more visible and easier for the consumer to access. We are also taking steps to help operators understand the reporting requirements better, which should make the reports we receive more consistent. Having said that we have been working hard, and will continue to work hard, to ensure that consumers are protected. In November we unveiled a new strategy which will help shape a well-regulated gambling market that works for consumers.”

In 2017, more than 7 out of 10 complaints among all gambling operators were in the “remote casino, betting and bingo” sector, which the UK Gambling Commission defines as online casino card games, slots, bingo and sports betting. Last year was the highest year on record with companies logging more than 100,000 complaints.

More than 1 out of 5 British people said they had gambled or placed a bet online once last year, according to the April 2018 UK Gambling Commission survey on gambling participation, which marks an increase of more than double from 2012. Online gambling accounted for 53 percent of reported gambling among those surveyed.

Remote Gambling in the UK on the rise

In the last three years, NHS surveys indicate that gambling in the UK has seen an overall marginal decrease of__ percent, but remote gambling has seen a small, but steady increase of __ percent . According to the Gambling Act of 2005, remote gambling includes television, phone, radio, online or “any other kind of electronic or other technology for facilitating communication”. As of February 2018, The Gambling Commission reports that laptops and mobile apps are the dominate mediums for remote gambling.

The prevalence of technology in the modern day has made gambling instant. Griffiths describes the accessibility of sports gambling to have transformed from a “discontinuous form of gambling into a continuous form of gambling”. Football bets used to be much more limited—often just one bet per match on the outcome. The Internet, however, has expanded the versatility of sports betting so that betting multiple times per match has become common practice.

The popularity of digital gambling is reflected in the gambling industry’s earnings. Between 2014 and 2016 the industry’s overall remote gambling revenue increased by 216.87 percent. However, during the same period, complaints about remote gambling increased by 1674.65 percent. This disparity indicates that popularity in online gambling is not the only contributing factor to the rise in complaints.

Technology has turned more forms of gambling into what is referred to as high event frequency gambling. In sports betting, for instance, on days that matches are streamed back to back, there is increased opportunities and reminders to place bets. Technology encourages continuous gambling by providing the gambling industry a growing platform for advertisements and, subsequently, users are subjected to increased exposure.

Advertising/Promotional offers and social responsibility

The practice of consistently exposing the public to frequent gambling advertisements and promotional offers risks customer exploitation. Both threaten to mislead the client, often featuring convoluted rhetoric frequent in legal documents. Promotional offers tend to be similarly formatted across the industry, so seasoned gamblers will be familiar with the vocabulary. However, flashy appearances and promising sums create a risk that newcomers will sign up without fully understanding the offer and the probability of winning. Such an environment raises legal and ethical concerns about the gambling industry’s practices.

In addition to UK’s advertising and media laws, The Gambling Commission and the Industry Group for Responsible Gambling (IGRG) have administered regulations specifically monitoring the ethical standards of the gambling industry. The introduction of IGRG’s Gambling industry code outlines that the aim is to “provide gambling operators with a range of measures that would enhance the social responsibility of their advertising and which went over and above the extensive requirements already placed on them by the gambling and advertising regulators”. Hayler does not believe there is a widespread issue of predatory behaviour necessarily, rather “there is a wide range of people taking part in these offers with a very broad range of skills and experience in the sector”.

Potential problem gamblers are exposed to the same advertisement experience. Griffiths explains that data collated by gambling companies contains the necessary information to flag at risk users, but is not utilised in that capacity. He argues that “the direct promoting of marketing and advertising materials to players who companies pretty much know are problem gamblers, to me that’s just ethically irresponsible”. It is apparent that the gambling industry has the means to foster a safe and equal environment for all gamblers. The issue that remains is not whether there is enough regulation in place, but whether it is enforced.
